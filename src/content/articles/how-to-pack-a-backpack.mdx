Most people pack a backpack by putting things in the order they think of them. This creates a heavy, poorly balanced load that strains your lower back, shifts unpredictably on descents, and forces you to unpack everything when you need something at the bottom. A systematic packing approach, based on where weight sits relative to your center of gravity, makes the same load significantly more comfortable to carry — and faster to access on trail.

## The three zones of a hiking pack

Think of your backpack in three vertical zones, from bottom to top, plus an accessibility layer closest to your back.

### Zone 1 — Bottom: light, bulky items

The bottom of the pack is for gear you won't need until camp:

- Sleeping bag (in a compression sack if it doesn't fill the space well)
- Camp clothes and layers you won't need during the hiking day
- Sleeping pad, if your pack has a sleeping pad sleeve or the pad fits vertically inside

**Why**: Heavy items here pull your center of gravity low and away from your back, which creates lower back fatigue and an unstable feel on uneven terrain.

### Zone 2 — Core: heavy items, close to your back

The middle zone, closest to your spine, is where your heaviest gear goes:

- Tent body and rainfly
- Food and bear canister (the heaviest single item for most trips)
- Water (1L of water weighs 1 kg — a significant portion of total pack weight)
- Stove and fuel

**Why**: Placing heavy items high and close to your spine moves weight directly over your hips and keeps the pack from pulling away from your back. This is the most important packing principle — heavy items near your back, centered mid-height.

### Zone 3 — Top: items needed during the day

The top of the main compartment is for gear you'll access without stopping to dig:

- Rain jacket
- First aid kit
- Snacks and trail food
- Sun protection
- Headlamp (see our [headlamp recommendations](/reviews/best-headlamps-camping))

### Hip belt pockets and top lid: fast-access items

- Phone, map, compass
- Sunscreen and lip balm
- Hand sanitiser and wipes
- Snacks for easy reach while moving

## Step-by-step packing process

**Step 1: Line the main compartment with a pack liner.**
A large garbage bag or dry bag inside the main compartment keeps everything dry when it rains — more reliable than relying on your pack's rain cover, which can blow off or shift. Do this before adding any gear.

**Step 2: Pack the sleeping bag.**
Compress it into its stuff sack and place it in the bottom of the main compartment. If your pack has a separate bottom sleeping bag compartment, use it.

**Step 3: Build the heavy core.**
Place your tent body against the back panel at mid-height. Stack food, fuel, and water directly behind the center of your back. These are your heaviest items and should sit as high and close to your spine as practical.

**Step 4: Fill around the core.**
Pack clothes, camp shoes, and other soft items around the heavy core to fill gaps. Soft items conform to odd shapes and are good filler.

**Step 5: Top-load daily access items.**
Rain gear, first aid, snacks — anything you'll need during the hiking day goes last in the main compartment so you can reach it without digging.

**Step 6: Attach external gear last.**
Tent poles go in external sleeve pockets (vertically, not sticking out horizontally). Trekking poles clip to the pack when not in use. Wet gear can be lashed to external lash points temporarily.

## Adjusting the fit before you walk

A correctly loaded pack is only comfortable if it's adjusted to your torso. The key adjustments:

1. **Loosen all straps** before putting the pack on
2. **Clip the hip belt** around your hips (over the iliac crest, not your waist — the bony protrusion is the landmark)
3. **Tighten the hip belt** until it feels snug — 60–80% of the weight should be on your hips, not shoulders
4. **Pull down and forward on the shoulder straps** until they conform to your shoulders without gaps
5. **Snug the load lifter straps** (small straps above the shoulder straps) at a 45-degree angle — don't overtighten, or they'll pull the pack off your hips
6. **Connect the sternum strap** across your chest; tighten enough to keep shoulder straps in position without restricting breathing

After walking for five minutes, adjust again. The pack will settle slightly as weight redistributes.

## Weight-saving mindset for long trips

Pack weight matters more the longer your trip. A useful heuristic: every item you add should earn its place by being used on every trip, or being critical enough that you'd regret not having it. 

Common weight-savers that don't require expensive gear purchases:
- Decant toiletries into small travel containers rather than bringing full-size bottles
- Share gear across a group (one tent, one stove, shared food)
- Check the weather forecast and only bring rain gear if needed rather than carrying it always
- Wear your heaviest clothing layers rather than packing them
