A good headlamp is one of those pieces of gear you don't think about until it fails at the worst possible moment. We ran seven headlamps through campsite cooking sessions, night hiking on uneven trail, cold-weather tent use, and emergency pack scenarios to identify what actually matters — and what specs are mostly marketing.

## What we tested and how

Testing covered three use scenarios across two months:

- **Campsite use**: cooking, setting up gear, reading in tent at close range
- **Trail hiking**: night hiking at 3–5 km/h pace on maintained trail, looking for obstacles, reading trail markers
- **Cold-weather use**: performance at 0°C and -10°C, including battery runtime comparisons

We paid attention to beam quality, not just lumen numbers — a wide, even flood beam is worth more for camp use than raw peak brightness, and most headlamps' advertised lumen figures are measured at turn-on, not sustained over runtime.

## Best overall: Black Diamond Spot 400

The Spot 400 is the headlamp that earns a permanent spot in most people's kit. It puts out 400 lumens on high, drops to a 10-lumen camp mode that won't blind your tent-mates, and includes a red night-vision mode that preserves your eyes' dark adaptation during nighttime navigation. The beam pattern is well-shaped — a useful central spot surrounded by enough flood spill to see where you're stepping without tunnel-vision.

Battery life is honest: you get about 3.5 hours at full brightness, but the low mode (which covers most camp use) lasts over 200 hours on three AAA batteries. The Spot 400 is also IPX8 waterproof (submersible to 1 metre), runs on readily available AAA batteries, and has a simple one-button operation with tap and hold controls that work with gloves.

The main limitations: it doesn't have a rechargeable battery (USB-C), so you're relying on buying AAA batteries. For day hikes and basecamp camping, this is rarely a problem. For multi-week expeditions where weight and rechargeability matter more, look at the Petzl option.

## Best rechargeable: Petzl Actik Core

The Actik Core solves the rechargeable vs. AAA dilemma by doing both. It ships with a proprietary lithium battery that charges via USB, but also accepts standard AAA batteries as a fallback — you can swap to AAAs mid-trip if the internal battery runs out. This hybrid design makes it genuinely useful for multi-day backcountry use where you can't guarantee a charge point.

At 450 lumens peak output, it's slightly brighter than the Spot 400. The beam pattern is similar — mixed flood/spot — and Petzl's CORE battery holds charge well between trips (useful for headlamps that sit in a kit bag for months between uses). Weight is 87g including the CORE battery, which is reasonable for the capability.

The trade-off: the CORE battery costs extra if you need a spare, and the switching interface takes a few uses to memorize. At close-quarters camp distances, it's occasionally easy to overshoot to a brightness level higher than you need.

## Best budget pick: Fenix HL32R-T

If you're outfitting a family campsite or want a capable spare headlamp without spending $40+, the Fenix HL32R-T delivers solid performance at a lower price. It outputs 800 lumens at max (useful for a short burst during camp setup), steps down to practical mid and low modes for sustained use, and charges via USB-C.

In testing, the HL32R-T ran reliably through two long camping weekends without issues. It lacks a dedicated red mode, which matters for nighttime navigation but is less critical for general campsite use. Build quality feels a small step below Black Diamond and Petzl, but for its price, it's hard to fault.

## What to look for when buying a headlamp

**Lumens vs. beam pattern**: A 200-lumen headlamp with a well-shaped beam illuminates a trail better than a 400-lumen light with a narrow hotspot. Look for demos or beam-shot photos when evaluating — raw lumen numbers are not a reliable quality indicator.

**Runtime at usable brightness**: Manufacturers often advertise runtime at the lowest brightness mode to hit big numbers. Check how long the headlamp runs at a mid-level brightness (200–300 lumens), since that's what you'll actually use on trail.

**Red light mode**: Red light preserves night vision — your eyes stay dark-adapted, so you can look up from your headlamp and still see the surroundings. Essential for navigating without blinding companions and useful for reading maps at night.

**IPX rating**: IPX4 means splash-resistant; IPX7 is waterproof to 1m for 30 minutes; IPX8 is fully submersible. For general camping and hiking in rain, IPX4 to IPX7 is sufficient. IPX8 matters primarily for water activities or if you drop gear in streams.

**Weight**: A headlamp that lives in a trail pack should be under 100g including batteries. For base-camp use, weight matters less than output and runtime.
