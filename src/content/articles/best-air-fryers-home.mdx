Air fryers became a kitchen staple because they genuinely deliver on their core promise: faster, crispier results than a conventional oven with far less oil than deep frying. But the category has expanded dramatically, and there's a wide range in cooking performance, capacity, and usability between models at similar price points.

We cooked over 40 batches across six models — french fries, chicken wings, frozen snacks, fresh vegetables, and reheated pizza — measuring crispiness, evenness, cook time, and how much variance existed between different positions in the basket.

## What actually matters in an air fryer

**Wattage**: Most air fryers run between 1,400W and 1,800W. Higher wattage means faster preheating and more consistent temperature maintenance when you open the basket and add cold food. For busy cooking, 1,700W+ is preferable.

**Basket vs. oven style**: Basket air fryers (traditional cylindrical design) are faster to heat and easier to shake food in. Oven-style models (toaster oven shape with a door) offer more capacity and the ability to cook on multiple racks simultaneously, but preheat more slowly and require flipping food rather than shaking.

**Basket coating**: Non-stick coatings on baskets make cleanup much easier, but cheaper PTFE coatings can scratch and degrade. Ceramic-coated baskets are more durable and considered safer at high temperatures. Check whether the basket is dishwasher-safe — it makes a meaningful difference in daily usability.

**Temperature range**: Most air fryers cover 80°C to 200°C (175°F to 400°F). Some extend to 230°C, which is useful for very crispy results on fries and wings. The low end (80–100°C) is useful for dehydrating or keeping food warm.

## Tips for better air fryer results

**Don't overcrowd the basket.** Air fryers work by circulating hot air around food — if the basket is packed, food steams instead of crisping. A single layer with space between pieces is the target; cook in batches if needed.

**Pat food dry before cooking.** Surface moisture is the enemy of crispiness. Pat chicken, fish, and vegetables dry with a paper towel before adding oil and seasoning.

**Use the right amount of oil.** Most foods need just 1–2 teaspoons of oil tossed through the food, not a spray on top. Foods that are already fatty (chicken thighs, salmon, bacon) often need no added oil at all.

**Preheat for 2–3 minutes.** Most air fryers benefit from a short preheat — you get more consistent results and the cook time is more predictable.
