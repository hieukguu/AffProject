Setting up a tent looks straightforward until you're doing it for the first time at dusk with poles that look identical to each other and instructions that assume you already know what you're doing. This guide covers the process from arriving at your campsite to being fully storm-ready — including the parts most setup guides skip.

## Before you leave home: practise the pitch

The single most valuable preparation step is setting up your tent in your backyard or living room before your first trip. This has three benefits:

1. You find out if any parts are missing (poles, stakes, guylines) before you're at the trailhead
2. You identify any setup steps that are confusing without time pressure
3. You confirm your tent has no defects in the floor or seams

Aim to set up and take down the tent twice before your first trip. The second setup is always faster, and the muscle memory transfers to field conditions.

---

## Step 1: Choose and prepare the site

**What to look for:**
- **Flat, level ground** — even a small slope becomes very noticeable when sleeping. Test by lying down in the direction you'll sleep before committing to the spot.
- **Natural drainage** — avoid the lowest point in a hollow or anywhere water could pool. A slight upward slope (1–2°) on the head side of the tent improves drainage.
- **Windbreak protection** — trees, rock formations, or ridgelines that block prevailing wind reduce tent noise and heat loss significantly.
- **No overhead hazards** — dead branches (widow-makers), overhanging rock, or anything that could fall on the tent overnight.

**What to avoid:**
- Digging a trench around the tent — this damages the campsite and is prohibited in most established campgrounds and backcountry zones. A properly placed footprint and correct rainfly tensioning handles drainage without site modification.
- Dry riverbeds or wash areas — these can fill rapidly in a rainstorm even if the sky is clear where you're camping.
- Areas with heavy insect activity at ground level (ant nests, mosquito-heavy wet ground).

**Clear the footprint area:**
Remove any rocks, sticks, pinecones, or roots that could puncture the tent floor or create uncomfortable lumps. This takes 3–5 minutes but saves hours of discomfort.

---

## Step 2: Lay down the footprint and orient the tent

Unfold the footprint and place it on your cleared area. The footprint should sit 5–10 cm inside the tent wall perimeter — never extending past the edge.

Orient the tent entrance based on:
- **Wind direction** — door opening away from prevailing wind
- **View** — if you want to see something specific from the tent
- **Morning sun** — east-facing doors catch morning light; west-facing catch afternoon heat

Place the tent body on the footprint. Identify which end is the head end if the tent is asymmetrical (most tents have a slightly wider or higher head area).

---

## Step 3: Assemble and thread the poles

Most modern camping tents use shock-corded poles (sections connected by internal elastic cord) that unfold like a linked chain. Identify all the pole sections and unfold them fully before threading.

**For dome tents (most common design):**
1. Thread the first pole diagonally from one corner grommet through the sleeve or clip system to the opposite corner
2. Thread the second pole diagonally across the first, creating the X-shape that gives the dome its structure
3. Insert all four pole ends into the corner grommet sockets to create tension — the tent should immediately begin to take shape

**Clips vs. sleeves:**
Many modern tents use clips instead of full sleeves — the pole runs through a track and clips hook over it. Clips are faster to set up; sleeves provide more weather protection at the pole contact points. Both work well.

**Don't force poles.** If a pole section won't connect smoothly, check for dirt or debris in the ferrule (the metal end fitting). A stuck pole forced into place can crack the shaft.

---

## Step 4: Stake the corners

Stakes serve two purposes: preventing the tent from moving and maintaining the floor tension that keeps the geometry correct.

Stake each corner at a **45° angle away from the tent**, with the stake head at ground level or just below. Staking vertically provides less hold — the angled stake resists pull in the direction of the tent wall.

On soft soil, stakes push in by hand or with a boot heel. On rocky or hard ground, use a rock or tent stake mallet. On sand, use sand-specific stakes (wider surface area) or bury rocks or gear bags as anchors.

---

## Step 5: Attach and tension the rainfly

The rainfly is the outer waterproof shell that goes over the tent body. Most attach at the corners (using hooks, velcro tabs, or buckles) and cover the tent body completely.

**Common mistakes with the rainfly:**

- **Not tensioning it enough**: A slack rainfly that touches the inner tent wall will transfer condensation directly to the inside of the tent during rain or temperature drops. The fly should have several centimetres of clearance from the inner tent on all sides.
- **Misaligning the fly**: The fly's door should align with the tent body's door. On most tents, this is obvious; on some symmetric tents, it's possible to put it on backwards.

Tension each corner strap until the fly is taut with no sagging panels.

## Step 6: Stake out the guylines

Guylines are the cord attachments on the rainfly that anchor the tent in high wind. In calm conditions you may not need them; in any wind over moderate strength, they're essential.

Run each guyline outward from the tent at roughly 45° and stake it at 2–3 tent-width distances from the tent wall. A taut guyline pulls the rainfly tighter, improves ventilation clearance, and prevents the tent from flexing excessively in gusts.

In a storm, pre-stake all guylines before conditions deteriorate — it's much harder to stake guylines in heavy rain with cold hands.

---

## Step 7: Check ventilation

Most three-season tents have mesh panels on the inner tent body and vents on the rainfly. With the tent fully set up, check that:

- The vestibule (outer door area) can be opened for airflow without letting rain in
- Any upper fly vents are open (unless it's very cold and you want heat retention)
- There's clearance between the inner and outer walls for air circulation

Good ventilation significantly reduces condensation buildup inside the tent overnight. Closing all vents traps humid air and causes dripping condensation even on dry nights.

---

## After your trip: dry and store correctly

Storing a wet or damp tent causes mold and degrades the floor's waterproof coating. After camping:

1. Air-dry the tent fully before packing away — even if it means setting it up again at home
2. Store loosely in a large bag or pillowcase, not compressed in the stuff sack (compression degrades the coatings over time)
3. Store in a cool, dry location away from direct sunlight (UV degrades tent materials)
