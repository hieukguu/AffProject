Robot vacuums have improved dramatically in the last three years — modern flagship models navigate accurately, avoid obstacles in real time, and empty themselves automatically. But there's still a wide gap between the best and worst in the category, and the marketing claims around suction power and smart mapping don't always match real-world performance.

We tested six models across an 8-week period in homes with hardwood floors, low-pile carpet, tile, and pets — the four surfaces that reveal the most about a robot vacuum's actual capability.

## What matters in a robot vacuum (and what doesn't)

**Suction power (Pa)**: Suction is measured in Pascals. Budget models offer 1,000–2,000 Pa; mid-range hits 2,500–4,000 Pa; flagships reach 5,000–10,000+ Pa. For hardwood and tile, 2,000 Pa is sufficient. For carpet, especially medium and high pile, you want 3,000 Pa minimum. Very high suction numbers (8,000+ Pa) are mostly relevant for embedded pet hair in carpet.

**Navigation type**: Older models use random bump-and-clean patterns. Modern models use LiDAR (laser) or camera-based mapping to build a floor plan and clean systematically. Systematic navigation covers more floor area in less time and avoids missing sections. It costs more, but for any home over 60 square meters, it's worth paying for.

**Brush roll design**: Rubber dual-roller systems handle pet hair without tangling. Traditional bristle brushes are cheaper but wrap with long hair and require frequent untangling. If you have pets or people with long hair in your household, this is a non-negotiable feature.

**Auto-empty base**: Models with self-emptying bases automatically transfer dustbin contents to a larger bag in the charging dock. The bag typically holds 30–60 dustbin loads, reducing how often you need to interact with the vacuum to once every few weeks.

The trade-off is cost — it's the most expensive model in this review — and the large base station footprint. For a single room or a small flat, this is overkill. For a full-size home, especially one with pets, it's the most hands-off cleaning solution available.

## Best for obstacle avoidance: iRobot Roomba j7+

The Roomba j7+ is built around one specific strength: avoiding objects on the floor that other robots drive through or get stuck on — pet waste, charging cables, small socks. iRobot calls this PrecisionVision, and in testing, it was the most reliable obstacle avoidance we encountered across all models tested. It correctly identified and avoided a phone charging cable, a dog chew toy, and a pair of shoes in every single test run.

Beyond obstacle avoidance, the j7+ offers solid performance on hardwood and low-pile carpet, 10x suction compared to Roomba 600 series, and a self-emptying base that holds about 60 days of debris. It maps efficiently and supports room assignment for targeted cleaning.

The limitation: suction is strong but not at the level of Roborock's flagship for heavy carpet and embedded pet hair. For homes primarily on hardwood or tile with occasional rugs, the j7+ is excellent. For homes with wall-to-wall carpet and heavy pet shedding, the Roborock wins on raw cleaning power.

## How to get the most out of a robot vacuum

**Run it daily, not weekly.** A robot vacuum run daily picks up a small amount of surface debris each time and keeps floors consistently clean. Run weekly, it has to deal with accumulated debris and pet hair clumps — which strains suction and fills the bin faster.

**Prepare the floor before running.** Pick up phone chargers, small clothing items, and pet toys before scheduling a clean. Even the best obstacle avoidance isn't perfect, and cables are the most common way robot vacuums get stuck or damaged.

**Clean the brush roll weekly.** Even rubber brush rolls accumulate hair over time. A 2-minute weekly clean prevents the motor from working harder and extends the brush roll's life.
